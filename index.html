THE STANDARD FOR PROJECT MANAGEMENT • CHAPTER 1

1 Introduction

This lesson follows the uploaded PMBOK® Guide, Eighth Edition in its published order.

1.1

Purpose of The Standard for Project Management

What this section establishes: The Standard provides a foundation for understanding project management and the relationships among projects, programs, portfolios, operations, governance, and value delivery.

The Standard is intended to apply across industries, organizational settings, delivery approaches, and project types. It is not a rigid recipe. It establishes concepts and expectations that practitioners tailor to context.

PMI thinking: Prefer context-sensitive application over one-size-fits-all process compliance.
Exam trap: Assuming the guide requires the same methods, documents, or governance intensity for every project.

Checkpoint

Which statement best reflects the purpose of the Standard?

1.2

Key Terms and Concepts

Chapter 1 introduces the core vocabulary used throughout the Standard. These terms are more than definitions; they form the logic behind PMP scenario questions.

ProjectA temporary endeavor undertaken to create a unique product, service, or result.
Project managementApplying knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques to project activities to meet objectives and deliver value.
DeliverableA unique and verifiable product, result, or capability required to complete work.
ValueBenefits—financial or nonfinancial—relative to the investment required.
StakeholderAn individual, group, or organization that can affect, be affected by, or perceive itself to be affected by the project.
GovernanceThe framework for authority, accountability, oversight, decision-making, and escalation.
PMP exam clue: If a scenario asks what to do FIRST, PMI often expects assessment, clarification, or stakeholder engagement before execution.
1.3

Foundational Elements of Project Management

This section connects the core ideas of project characteristics, governance, operations, and organizational work systems.

Strategy & Governance→Portfolios & Programs→Projects→Operations→Outcomes & Value
Remember: Projects do not exist in isolation. They are part of a larger organizational system for delivering value.
1.3.1

Characteristics of a Project

Temporary

A project has a defined beginning and ending. Temporary does not mean short.

Unique

The project creates a unique product, service, result, or combination of these.

Projects may produce tangible or intangible deliverables. The project may end, but the deliverable and its benefits can continue.

Example: A system implementation project ends after transition and acceptance. The system then moves into ongoing operations.
Exam trap: Confusing repetitive work performed during a project with operations. Repetition inside a temporary endeavor does not automatically make the endeavor operational.
1.3.2

Connecting Organizational Governance and Project Governance

Organizational governance provides the broader rules, authority, and strategic direction. Project governance applies those expectations to the project’s specific decisions and oversight.

Organizational governance

  • Strategic direction
  • Policies and authority
  • Investment oversight
  • Enterprise accountability

Project governance

  • Decision rights
  • Escalation paths
  • Approvals and controls
  • Project accountability
PMI thinking: When authority is unclear, use the governance framework or escalation path—do not guess or act outside delegated authority.
Exam trap: Treating governance as bureaucracy to bypass. Proper governance protects alignment and value.
1.3.3

Operations and Project Management

Projects create or change capabilities. Operations sustain and use those capabilities. Effective transition requires involvement from operational stakeholders before project closure.

Projects

  • Temporary
  • Create change
  • Produce unique outcomes

Operations

  • Ongoing
  • Sustain the organization
  • Use and support delivered capabilities
Exam clue: When a project will hand off a product or service, involve operations early and plan knowledge transfer, acceptance, support, and ownership.
1.3.4

Relationship of Portfolio, Program, Project, and Operations Management

These forms of management have different purposes but interact to deliver organizational value.

PortfolioSelects and balances programs, projects, and operations to achieve strategy and maximize value.
ProgramCoordinates related projects and activities to obtain benefits not available when managed separately.
ProjectCreates a unique product, service, or result through a temporary endeavor.
OperationsSustains ongoing products, services, and organizational functions.

Chapter 1 Master Summary

  • The Standard provides broadly applicable, tailorable guidance.
  • Projects are temporary and unique; operations are ongoing.
  • Project governance connects project decisions to organizational governance.
  • Portfolios align investments with strategy; programs coordinate related projects.
  • Projects often transfer deliverables, knowledge, and ownership to operations.
